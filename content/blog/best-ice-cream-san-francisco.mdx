San Francisco has the most concentrated collection of excellent ice cream per square mile of any American city. This is not hyperbole — it's the result of a decade-long artisan food revolution, access to extraordinary Northern California dairy, proximity to the best produce in the country, and a population that takes eating seriously.

This guide focuses on the shops that are worth going out of your way for.

## The Mission District: Ground Zero for SF Ice Cream

The Mission District gave birth to San Francisco's modern ice cream scene. Two shops sit within blocks of each other, and visiting both in one afternoon is one of the city's great free pleasures (well, not free — but worth every dollar).

### Bi-Rite Creamery

At 3692 18th Street, Bi-Rite is the shop that started everything. The **salted caramel** is still the benchmark — rich, deep, and finished with French fleur de sel that you can actually taste. The **honey lavender** and **ricanelas** (cinnamon with snickerdoodle) are the second and third must-tries.

The line runs down the block on weekends and often extends into Dolores Park. Arrive by opening (11am) or accept the wait as part of the experience. The park is across the street; it's a good line to be in.

### Humphry Slocombe

Four blocks away at 2790 Harrison Street, Humphry Slocombe is the weirder, darker sibling. The **Secret Breakfast** — bourbon and Corn Flakes — is the cult classic. But the real game is checking the daily specials: previous menus have included foie gras ice cream, Thai chili lime, and beet molasses.

If Secret Breakfast is your first stop, order it. Then ask what's unusual today. Then get that too.

## Hayes Valley: The Liquid Nitrogen Corner

**Smitten Ice Cream** on Octavia Street makes ice cream to order with liquid nitrogen. The fresh-churned texture is genuinely different — smoother, denser, more intensely flavored than anything that's been stored. The seasonal specials change weekly based on what's available from local farms.

Come in summer for the brown butter peach. Come in winter for the hot fudge sundae. Come any time for the vanilla as a benchmark.

## Fillmore Street: The Neighborhood Strip

**Salt & Straw** on Fillmore is the California outpost of the beloved Portland brand. Their seasonal menus are worth planning around — the Thanksgiving menu (including turkey and gravy ice cream, which actually works) generates national press every November. The strawberry honey balsamic with black pepper is the year-round calling card.

The Fillmore location has some of the best late-night hours in the city (11pm most nights, midnight on weekends), which makes it the natural conclusion to an evening at the Fillmore music venue two blocks north.

## The Ferry Building: The Best View

**Smitten Ice Cream** has a second location at the Ferry Building. The ice cream is identical to Hayes Valley, but the setting is different: eat your scoop on the Embarcadero waterfront, watch the Bay Bridge, and understand why San Francisco has an ice cream culture that other cities can't replicate.

## Planning Your SF Ice Cream Day

**Half-day route (3 hours):**
- Start: Bi-Rite Creamery (Mission, 11am opening)
- Walk to Dolores Park and eat your scoop
- Walk to Humphry Slocombe (4 blocks)
- Muni N-Judah to Hayes Valley (25 min)
- Smitten Ice Cream on Octavia

**Full day:**
Add Salt & Straw on Fillmore for the afternoon (take the 22 bus) and end the day at the Ferry Building Smitten for sunset.

## Tips for Visiting SF Ice Cream Shops

**Weather note:** San Francisco is cold and foggy on summer afternoons. The best ice cream weather is September and October, when the fog retreats and the city enjoys its warmest temperatures. Locals call this "second summer."

**Parking:** Don't drive to the Mission. Take BART to 16th Street Mission. For Hayes Valley, take any Muni that stops near Market and Octavia.

**Lines:** Weekend afternoons (1pm–4pm) have the longest waits. Opening time and evenings after 7pm are generally shorter.
