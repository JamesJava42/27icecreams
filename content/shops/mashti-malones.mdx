## The History of Bastani in LA

Mashti (pronounced mash-tee) opened his ice cream shop on La Brea in 1980, shortly after the Iranian Revolution brought a wave of Persian immigrants to Los Angeles. The flavor he led with was bastani — traditional Persian ice cream made with rose water, saffron, and mastic (a resin that gives the ice cream its characteristic chewy, stretchy texture).

## What Makes It Different

Traditional bastani has a texture unlike anything in the Western ice cream tradition. The mastic (called gum arabic) and salep (orchid flour) give it a stretchy, taffy-like quality. This is ice cream you can pull — it forms strings if you bite slowly. The frozen cream nuggets folded in (called akbar mashti) are traditional and add textural punctuation.

## The Saffron

Iranian saffron — the world's finest — gives the bastani its golden hue and a subtle floral complexity beneath the rose water. Mashti uses real saffron, which you can tell immediately from the color and depth of flavor. It is not the watery yellow of saffron-flavored shortcuts.

## The Late Night Institution

Mashti Malone's is open until midnight every night. In a city of late-night food culture, this makes it the ideal destination after a Hollywood show, a night at the Bowl, or a late movie. The neighborhood is deeply Hollywood, and the mix of after-theater crowds with Farsi-speaking families is quintessentially LA.
